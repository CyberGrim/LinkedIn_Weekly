## Week 1
### Post
```
Old game code is like an archaeological site. Reading through old Jedi Academy source code, and the developer comments stopped me cold.
Almost every single one was pure frustration, exhaustion, or dark humour, all baked into the commits and the codebase forever. My personal favourite being the classic "i = 0x5f3759df - ( i >> 1 ); // what the f---?"
It reminded me of how archaeologists use graffiti scratched into temple walls to understand how people REALLY felt. Not the cleaned and polished versions we're meant to see.
Game development, and especially crunch culture, leaves scars you can actually read years later.
With the influx of generative AI and "only leave comments where the code doesn't explain itself" prompts, are we going to sanitise away the only honest record of what it actually cost to make the games we love?
```

Watching tonight's State of Play, I realised these showcase events still excite me just as much as they did 10+ years ago, staying up late into the morning to watch E3, getting more and more excited with every announcement.
Lately, I've seen more and more friends in the industry grow jaded. Some have lost interest in actually playing games altogether. Maybe life got in the way. Maybe the industry wore them down. I'm not sure.
What I do know is that I don't want that for myself. As difficult as this industry is to love right now, I still love it; playing games, watching them, and being part of bringing them to life.
Do you still play? What keeps bringing you back? And if you've stepped away, what happened? Did anything from tonight's State of Play get you excited too?

## Week 3
### Post
```
One of the parts of the build engineering lifecycle that I genuinely love is getting platforms ready to go out the door, whether that's for full release or just an internal playtest.
It's a strange kind of puzzle. Every platform has its own rules and quirks around things like DLC and game slices (betas, alphas, etc), hardly any of which make any sense. It's barely ever documented in a way that fully prepares you. You learn by doing, by hitting the problems and fixing them. Making a screen full of failed builds finally start going green will always be satisfying to me.
And here's the thing about this work: when it goes well, nobody notices. People just play the game. No fanfare, no acknowledgement, just the absence of a problem. It's only when something breaks that it becomes visible, and usually not in a good way.
I've come to genuinely enjoy that invisibility. There's something satisfying about doing the work that makes all the creativity of all the talented people I've been fortunate to work with visible and playable to the world at large.
What are some other parts of the games industry that you love, but don't get talked about too much (or only when something breaks)?
```
